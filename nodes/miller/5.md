You make ready at the river's edge, checking your gear one final time while your horse drinks quietly beside you. The road has gradually given way to soft mud and reeds, and the broad river now guides you towards Brookdown Crossing. If the Passing Stones are to be found anywhere, your search must surely begin here. And if not, there will at least be food, supplies and news to gather before you continue your journey.

Following the gentle current, you walk along the riverbank as the town slowly comes into view. Timber-framed buildings stand beside busy watermills, their great wheels turning steadily as the river powers workshops, mills and forges. It is a curious place where the old world and the new meet. Magic still lingers in the air like the scent of wildflowers after rain, while clever machines hum quietly beside ancient cottages. Here, enchantment and ingenuity have learned—mostly—to live side by side.

A graceful swan glides downstream ahead of you. Perched upon its broad back, no larger than sparrows, a cheerful gathering of fairy folk chatter and laugh amongst themselves, their bright wings catching the morning light. It is a delightful sight, and one that brings an smile to your face.

As the swan approaches a riverside watermill, the current quickens and you can see that all is not well. The bird beats its wings in surprise as the flow catches it sooner than expected, sweeping it towards the turning wheel. The startled fairies cling tightly to its feathers as bird and passengers disappear briefly beneath the churning spray. A moment later they emerge on the far side, washed clear of the wheel scattered across the water. The swan drifts awkwardly, clearly injured, while the tiny fairy folk struggle to keep themselves afloat.

You wade into the river until you reach them. Gently lifting the swan with one arm, you gather the dazed fairies with the other before carrying them safely back to the riverbank.

This can not be. The worlds of magic and logic must exist side by side, not destroy each other. Kneeling beside the swan, you close your eyes and draw upon a small measure of your precious magic.

Warmth flows from your hands like sunlight after rain. The swan gives a soft shiver before lifting its head once more. Around it, the fairy folk begin to stir, their delicate glow returning until their tiny wings hum once again. One by one they take to the air. You watch them disappear into the morning light before turning your attention towards the busy watermill.
